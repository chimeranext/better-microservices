# ISO 22005:2007 -- Traceability in the Feed and Food Chain

**Standard:** ISO 22005:2007  
**Full title:** Traceability in the feed and food chain -- General principles and basic requirements for system design and implementation  
**Published:** 2007  
**Technical Committee:** ISO/TC 34, Food products  
**Status:** Published (last reviewed and confirmed in 2016)  
**Pages:** 9

---

## 1. Scope

ISO 22005:2007 gives the principles and specifies the basic requirements for the design and implementation of a feed and food traceability system. It can be applied by an organization operating at any step in the feed and food chain. The standard is intended to be flexible enough to allow each organization to achieve its identified objectives.

The standard uses the same definition of traceability as the Codex Alimentarius Commission (CAC) and provides a complement for organizations implementing ISO 22000 (Food safety management systems).

The standard clarifies that traceability is a **technical tool** supporting food safety management but is insufficient on its own to achieve food safety. It complements rather than replaces systems like HACCP and ISO 22000.

## 3. Key Definitions

### 3.1 Traceability
The ability to follow the movement of a feed or food through specified stage(s) of production, processing and distribution.

### 3.2 Feed and food chain
Sequence of the stages and operations involved in the production, processing, distribution, storage and handling of a feed or food and its ingredients, from primary production to consumption.

### 3.3 Lot identification
The assignment of unique codes to product groups sharing common characteristics for traceability purposes.

### 3.4 Chain of custody
The documented trail of each transfer or transformation of a product from its origin through each step to the final consumer.

> **Note:** ISO 22005 aligns its traceability definition with the Codex Alimentarius Commission, ensuring international regulatory coherence.

## 4. General Principles

Traceability systems shall:

1. Be able to document the **history** of the product and/or locate a product in the feed and food chain
2. Contribute to the search for the **cause of nonconformity** and the ability to withdraw and/or recall products if necessary
3. Address at minimum **one step forward and one step backward** for each organization in the chain (the "one-up-one-down" principle)
4. Be **verifiable**, applied consistently and equitably
5. Be **results oriented**
6. Be **cost effective**
7. Be **practical to apply**
8. Comply with applicable **regulations and policies**

Movement documentation can relate to the origin of the materials, processing history, or distribution of the feed or food.

## 5. Design Requirements (Clause 5)

The design of a traceability system shall include the following nine essential elements:

### 5.1 Objectives
Clearly identify intended traceability goals, which may include:
- Regulatory compliance
- Rapid recall capability
- Fraud prevention
- Quality assurance
- Food safety support

### 5.2 Regulatory and policy requirements
Identify applicable mandates across all jurisdictions, including:
- FDA FSMA (United States)
- EU General Food Law Regulation 178/2002 (European Union)
- Origin labeling requirements
- National and regional food safety legislation

### 5.3 Products and/or ingredients
Define which items require tracing and at what granularity level. Specify the products, ingredients, and product contact materials included in the system scope.

### 5.4 Position in the feed and food chain
Understand the organization's location within the broader feed and food chain and identify immediate upstream (suppliers) and downstream (customers) partners.

### 5.5 Flow of materials
Document how inputs move through operations, including:
- Transformations
- Combinations
- Divisions
- Storage
- Distribution

### 5.6 Information requirements
Specify data to be captured at each step, including:
- Product identification
- Batch/lot codes
- Dates (production, processing, distribution)
- Quantity
- Origin
- Chain of custody details
- Supplier information
- Customer information

### 5.7 Procedures
Establish documented processes for:
- Product definition
- Lot identification
- Documentation management
- Data management
- Linking inputs to outputs
- Trace-back capability (from consumer to origin)
- Trace-forward capability (from origin to consumer)

### 5.8 Documentation
Maintain comprehensive system documentation, including:
- System design documentation
- Procedures
- Material flow diagrams
- Batch records
- Supplier information
- Production records

### 5.9 Feed and food chain coordination
Establish communication protocols with trading partners regarding:
- Identification schemes
- Information exchange requirements
- Data formats and systems interoperability

## 6. Implementation Requirements (Clause 6)

Organizations shall establish:

### 6.1 Traceability plan
- A documented plan with assigned responsibilities
- Clear allocation of resources

### 6.2 Personnel competence
- Training programs demonstrating competence
- Awareness of traceability procedures and their importance

### 6.3 Monitoring and measurement
- Monitoring schemes with key performance indicators (KPIs)
- Measurement of system effectiveness

### 6.4 Internal audits
- Audits at planned intervals to verify system conformity and effectiveness

### 6.5 Management review
- Regular review processes including:
  - Evaluation of audit results
  - Assessment of system performance
  - Corrective actions for identified nonconformities
  - Continuous improvement initiatives

---

## 7. Traceability Approaches

### 7.1 One-Up-One-Down Model
Each organization maintains links to its immediate supplier and customer only. This meets minimum EU and many regulatory standards requirements. It is simpler to implement but provides slower incident response.

### 7.2 Full Chain Model
A single organization maintains visibility across the entire supply chain from origin to consumer. More complex to implement but enables faster incident response and more precise recall scope minimization.

---

## 8. Recall and Withdrawal Capability

An effective traceability system enables:
- Rapid identification of affected product lots
- Determination of distribution locations
- Precise recall scope minimization (reducing waste and cost)
- Supply chain and consumer communication
- Recovery verification

---

## 9. Technology Considerations

The standard remains **technology-neutral** but common implementations include:
- GS1 barcodes and 2D codes
- RFID for automated data capture
- ERP and MES systems for lot tracking
- Cloud-based platforms for multi-partner data sharing
- Blockchain for tamper-resistant record-keeping (emerging)

## 11. Special Considerations

### Feed chains
Addresses unique feed industry challenges including complex formulations, significant processing transformations, and linkage to animal-derived food safety.

### Supply chain complexity
Acknowledges challenges from:
- Global supply chains with multiple jurisdictions
- Biological variability of raw materials
- Product transformation during processing
- Co-mingling of sources
- Distributed custody across multiple organizations

## 12. Applicable Industries and Organizations

- Food manufacturers
- Feed manufacturers
- Primary producers (farms, fisheries, harvesters)
- Retailers and distributors
- Food service operators
- Importers/exporters
- Organizations pursuing ISO 22000 or FSSC 22000 certification
- Companies making origin claims (organic, fair trade, geographic indication)
- Any organization in the feed or food chain

# ISO 28004-2:2014 -- Guidelines for Adopting ISO 28000 for Seaport Operations

**Standard:** ISO 28004-2:2014  
**Full title:** Security management systems for the supply chain -- Guidelines for the implementation of ISO 28000 -- Part 2: Guidelines for adopting ISO 28000 for use in medium and small seaport operations  
**Published:** 2014 (replaces ISO/PAS 28004-2:2012)  
**Technical Committee:** ISO/TC 8, Ships and marine technology (originally); transferred to ISO/TC 292, Security and resilience  
**Status:** Published (last reviewed and confirmed in 2022)  
**Type:** Guidelines (not a requirements/certifiable standard -- supports ISO 28000 certification)

---

## 1. Scope

ISO 28004-2:2014 provides guidelines for medium and small seaport operations that wish to adopt ISO 28000 as their security management system. It identifies:

- Supply chain **risk and threat scenarios** relevant to seaport operations
- Procedures for conducting **risk/threat assessments** specific to port environments
- **Evaluation criteria** for measuring conformance and effectiveness of documented security plans in accordance with ISO 28000 and the ISO 28004 series implementation guidelines

The standard is designed to help ports that may lack the resources of large international ports to implement a systematic, internationally recognized security management system.

## 3. Key Definitions

ISO 28004-2 relies on definitions from ISO 28000 and ISO 28004-1. Key terms relevant to seaport context include:

### 3.1 Security management system
A system of coordinated policies, processes, and practices through which a seaport manages its security objectives across all operations.

### 3.2 Supply chain integrity
The assurance that cargo processed through the seaport has not been tampered with, diverted, or compromised during port operations.

### 3.3 Level of confidence rating
A measurable assessment (scale of 1 to 5) indicating the degree of conformance of a seaport's security operations with ISO 28000 requirements for protecting supply chain integrity.

### 3.4 Threat scenario
A defined situation describing a potential security event, its method of execution, the assets at risk, and the potential consequences for seaport operations and supply chain integrity.

## 4. Confidence Rating System

A core output of ISO 28004-2 is the **level of confidence rating system** based on the quality of security management plans and procedures implemented by the seaport.

### Rating Scale (1 to 5)

| Level | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **1** | Minimal conformance -- basic security awareness exists but no systematic management |
| **2** | Partial conformance -- some procedures documented but inconsistently applied |
| **3** | Substantial conformance -- documented SMS with evidence of implementation |
| **4** | High conformance -- effective SMS with monitoring, measurement, and improvement |
| **5** | Full conformance -- mature SMS fully aligned with ISO 28000, continuously improved |

> **Note:** The exact descriptions for each level are defined within the standard. The table above reflects the general intent as described in publicly available summaries. [Exact level descriptions require ISO purchase]

The rating is used as a means of identifying a measurable level of confidence that the seaport security operations are in conformance with ISO 28000 for protecting the integrity of the supply chain cargo being processed by the seaport.

## 5. Risk and Threat Assessment Framework

### 5.1 Supply chain risk scenarios
The standard identifies risk and threat scenarios specific to medium and small seaport operations, including:

- **Cargo theft and pilferage** during loading, unloading, and storage
- **Smuggling** of contraband, weapons, or persons through port facilities
- **Terrorism** targeting port infrastructure, vessels, or cargo
- **Cyber-attacks** on port management systems, tracking software, or communications
- **Sabotage** of port equipment, utilities, or navigational aids
- **Unauthorized access** to restricted areas, vessels, or cargo storage
- **Environmental contamination** from intentional or accidental release of hazardous materials
- **Supply chain disruption** from labor actions, natural disasters, or equipment failure

### 5.2 Risk/threat assessment procedures
Organizations shall conduct assessments covering:

- **Asset identification:** Port infrastructure, vessels, cargo, information systems, personnel
- **Threat identification:** Internal and external threats based on location, cargo types, and geopolitical context
- **Vulnerability analysis:** Weaknesses in physical, procedural, and technological controls
- **Impact assessment:** Consequences of security events on operations, supply chain, and stakeholders
- **Likelihood determination:** Probability of threat scenarios based on intelligence and historical data
- **Risk prioritization:** Ranking risks by severity and likelihood to allocate resources effectively

## 6. Implementation Guidelines

### 6.1 Alignment with ISO 28000 clause structure
ISO 28004-2 provides seaport-specific guidance mapped to each requirement of ISO 28000:

#### Context of the organization (ISO 28000 Clause 4)
- Understand the port's role within the broader supply chain
- Identify all stakeholders: shipping lines, customs, port authority, terminal operators, trucking companies, warehousing, government agencies
- Map cargo flows through the port
- Identify applicable regulations: ISPS Code, national maritime security regulations, customs requirements

#### Leadership (ISO 28000 Clause 5)
- Port management commitment to security
- Definition of security policy appropriate to port size and operations
- Assignment of port security officer and security organization

#### Planning (ISO 28000 Clause 6)
- Port-specific risk assessment using the threat scenarios defined in the standard
- Setting measurable security objectives for port operations
- Planning for changes in port operations, cargo types, or threat levels

#### Support (ISO 28000 Clause 7)
- Security personnel resourcing and training
- Equipment procurement (surveillance, access control, screening)
- Communication protocols with maritime authorities, law enforcement, and supply chain partners
- Documentation management

#### Operation (ISO 28000 Clause 8)
- Access control for port facilities, restricted areas, and vessels
- Cargo inspection and verification procedures
- Container seal integrity management
- Vessel security coordination
- Emergency response procedures for security incidents
- Coordination with the International Ship and Port Facility Security (ISPS) Code requirements

#### Performance evaluation (ISO 28000 Clause 9)
- Security monitoring and measurement through the confidence rating system
- Internal security audits
- Port security management review
- Application of the 1-to-5 confidence rating for self-assessment and external evaluation

#### Improvement (ISO 28000 Clause 10)
- Incident investigation and lessons learned
- Corrective action for security nonconformities
- Continual improvement of port security operations

## 7. Specific Considerations for Medium and Small Ports

### 7.1 Resource constraints
The standard acknowledges that medium and small ports may have:
- Limited security budgets and personnel
- Less sophisticated technology infrastructure
- Shared facilities and multi-use areas
- Fewer dedicated security resources

### 7.2 Scalable approach
ISO 28004-2 provides a scalable framework allowing ports to:
- Implement security measures proportionate to their risk profile
- Prioritize high-impact, low-cost controls
- Build security capability progressively
- Leverage the confidence rating system to demonstrate improvement over time

### 7.3 Regulatory alignment
The standard supports compliance with:
- **ISPS Code** (International Ship and Port Facility Security Code)
- National port security regulations
- Customs security programs (C-TPAT, AEO)
- Local law enforcement requirements
